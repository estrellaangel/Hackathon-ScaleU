What is a Surprise Bill?

A surprise medical bill (also called "balance billing") occurs when you receive care from an out-of-network provider at an in-network facility, or during an emergency when you can't choose your provider. You thought you'd pay in-network rates, but instead get billed for the full amount.

‚ö†Ô∏è Common Surprise Bill Scenarios

1. Emergency Room Visit

You go to an in-network ER, but the doctor treating you is out-of-network. You get a $3,000 surprise bill.

2. Surgery at In-Network Hospital

Your surgeon and hospital are in-network, but the anesthesiologist is out-of-network. You're billed $2,500 unexpectedly.

3. Lab Work Sent Out-of-Network

Your in-network doctor orders lab tests but sends them to an out-of-network lab without telling you. Bill: $800.

4. Assistant Surgeon You Didn't Choose

During planned surgery, an out-of-network assistant surgeon helps without your knowledge or consent. Surprise bill: $1,500.

The No Surprises Act

Federal Law ‚Ä¢ Effective January 1, 2022

This federal law protects you from most surprise medical bills in emergency and certain non-emergency situations.

‚úì You're Protected When:
  • You receive emergency care at any facility (in or out-of-network)
  • You get care from an out-of-network provider at an in-network facility
  • Air ambulance services transport you
‚úì What It Means for You:
  • You only pay your in-network cost-sharing amount (copay/coinsurance)
  • Out-of-network providers can't bill you for the balance
  • Your out-of-pocket costs count toward your in-network deductible

8 Ways to Avoid Surprise Medical Bills

1

Always Verify In-Network Status

Before any scheduled procedure or appointment, call your insurance to confirm the provider, facility, lab, and any specialists are all in-network.

2

Ask About ALL Providers Involved

For surgeries, ask about the anesthesiologist, radiologist, pathologist, and any assistants. Request in-network providers for each role.

3

Get Pre-Authorization for Procedures

Some procedures require pre-approval from your insurer. Getting this beforehand prevents coverage denials and surprise costs.

4

Request Cost Estimates in Writing

Ask the facility for a "Good Faith Estimate" of all expected costs before scheduled services. This is your right under federal law.

5

Check Lab & Imaging Centers

If your doctor orders lab work or X-rays, verify the lab/imaging center is in-network before going.

6

Review Bills Carefully

Check every medical bill for errors, duplicate charges, or services you didn't receive. Billing mistakes are very common.

7

Keep Detailed Records

Save all insurance cards, bills, EOBs (Explanation of Benefits), and correspondence. These are crucial if you need to dispute a bill.

8

Know Your Appeal Rights

If you receive a surprise bill, you have the right to appeal. Contact your insurance company immediately to dispute the charge.

What to Do If You Get a Surprise Bill

‚ö†Ô∏è Don't Panic and Don't Pay Immediately!

You have rights and options. Follow these steps:

1

Don't Pay the Bill Yet

Wait for an Explanation of Benefits (EOB) from your insurance before paying anything. The bill amount often changes after insurance processing.

2

Call Your Insurance Company

Contact your insurer immediately and explain the situation. Reference the No Surprises Act if applicable. Ask them to reprocess the claim at in-network rates.

3

Contact the Provider's Billing Department

Call the provider who sent the bill. Explain you thought you'd pay in-network rates. Ask them to accept your insurance's in-network payment.

4

File a Formal Appeal

Submit a written appeal to your insurance within the timeframe specified in your EOB (usually 180 days). Include all documentation.

5

Request External Review

If your appeal is denied, you can request an independent external review. This is free and binding on your insurance company.

6

Contact State Regulators

If all else fails, file a complaint with your state's Department of Insurance or the federal No Surprises Help Desk (1-800-985-3059).
