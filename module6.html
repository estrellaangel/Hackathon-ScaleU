Mental Health Care is Health Care

Your mental health is just as important as your physical health. Thanks to the Mental Health Parity Act, insurance companies must cover mental health services at the same level as medical/surgical services. You have the right to affordable mental health care.

Types of Mental Health Professionals

Psychiatrist (MD/DO)

Medical doctors specializing in mental health. Can prescribe medications and provide therapy.

Best for: Medication management, complex mental health conditions, dual diagnosis (mental health + substance use)
üí∞ Typical copay: $30-60 per visit

Psychologist (PhD/PsyD)

Doctoral-level mental health professionals. Provide therapy and psychological testing but cannot prescribe medication.

Best for: Talk therapy, cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT), psychological assessments, trauma treatment
üí∞ Typical copay: $25-50 per session

Licensed Therapist (LCSW, LPC, LMFT)

Master's-level counselors and therapists. Provide individual, couples, and family therapy.

Best for: Talk therapy, relationship issues, stress management, coping strategies, life transitions
üí∞ Typical copay: $20-40 per session

University Counseling Center

On-campus mental health services available to students, often at no cost or low cost.

Best for: Academic stress, peer relationships, identity exploration, adjustment issues, crisis support
üí∞ Typical cost: FREE or minimal fees for students

How to Access Mental Health Care

1

Check Your Insurance Coverage

Verify your plan covers mental health services (almost all do). Check copays for therapy visits and whether you need a referral from your primary care doctor.

2

Start with Your University Counseling Center

Most colleges offer free or low-cost counseling sessions. This is often the quickest way to get help and they can refer you for ongoing care if needed.

3

Find an In-Network Provider

Use your insurance company's provider directory to find therapists or psychiatrists who accept your insurance.

Popular directories:
  • Psychology Today therapist finder
  • Your insurance company's website
  • Zocdoc for mental health providers
4

Consider Teletherapy

Online therapy is convenient, often more affordable, and widely covered by insurance. Platforms like BetterHelp, Talkspace, and your insurer's telehealth services connect you with licensed therapists via video.

5

Schedule Your First Appointment

Call or book online. Have your insurance card ready. The first session is typically an assessment where you discuss your concerns and goals.

Your Privacy is Protected

What's Confidential

  • Everything you discuss in therapy is private and protected by law (HIPAA)
  • Your therapist cannot share information without your written consent
  • Your parents, school, or employer cannot access your records without permission
  • Insurance companies only receive basic billing info, not session details

When Therapists Must Break Confidentiality

Therapists are legally required to report only in these situations:

  • You're in immediate danger of harming yourself
  • You plan to harm someone else
  • Child or elder abuse is occurring
  • A court orders release of records (rare)

Common Concerns About Seeking Mental Health Care

‚ùì "I'm not sure if my problems are 'serious enough' for therapy."

You don't need to be in crisis to benefit from therapy. If something is bothering you or affecting your life, that's enough reason to seek support.

‚ùì "I'm worried about the cost."

Most insurance plans cover therapy with copays as low as $20-40. University counseling centers often provide free sessions. Ask AIDed to check your specific coverage.

‚ùì "Will this go on my permanent record?"

No. Mental health treatment is confidential and does not appear on academic records, background checks, or employment applications unless you choose to disclose it.

‚ùì "What if I don't connect with my first therapist?"

It's completely normal to "shop around" for a therapist. Finding the right fit is important, and it's okay to try someone new if the first doesn't feel right.

‚ùì "Will my parents find out?"

If you're 18 or older, your mental health information is private by law. However, if you're on a parent's insurance plan, they may see billing statements showing you used mental health benefits (but not details of what was discussed).

üí° Tips for Your First Therapy Session

Be honest and open

Your therapist can only help if they understand what you're going through.

It's okay to feel nervous

Almost everyone feels anxious before their first session. Therapists expect this!

Come prepared with what you want to work on

Think about your goals for therapy, but it's also okay to say "I'm not sure where to start."

Ask questions

Ask about the therapist's approach, what to expect, and how often you should meet.

Give it time

Therapy takes time to work. Commit to at least 4-6 sessions before deciding if it's helping.
