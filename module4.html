Understanding Drug Tiers

Insurance companies organize prescription medications into "tiers" based on cost. Lower tiers have lower copays, while higher tiers cost more out of pocket.

Tier 1 - Preferred Generic

$5-15 copay

Most affordable option. These are generic medications that work just as well as brand-name drugs.

Examples: Generic antibiotics, blood pressure medications, antidepressants

Tier 2 - Generic

$20-40 copay

Still generic, but less commonly prescribed or slightly more expensive to produce.

Examples: Generic extended-release formulations, certain generics

Tier 3 - Preferred Brand

$50-80 copay

Brand-name drugs on your insurer's preferred list, or generics with no Tier 1/2 alternative.

Examples: Common brand-name drugs, some specialty medications

Tier 4 - Non-Preferred Brand

$100-200+ copay

Most expensive option. Brand-name drugs not on the preferred list.

Examples: Newer brand-name drugs, non-preferred specialty medications

Tier 5 - Specialty

25-33% coinsurance

High-cost medications for complex conditions. Often require special handling.

Examples: Cancer medications, biologics, drugs requiring refrigeration

What is a Formulary?

A formulary is your insurance plan's list of covered prescription drugs. It determines which medications are covered and how much you'll pay.

‚úÖ On Formulary

Drugs listed on the formulary are covered by your insurance at the tier pricing.

Example: Your antibiotic is Tier 1 ‚Üí $10 copay

‚ùå Not on Formulary

Drugs NOT on the formulary may not be covered at all, or you'll pay full price.

Example: Brand-name drug not covered ‚Üí $300 out-of-pocket

10 Ways to Save Money on Prescriptions

1. Always ask for generic versions

Generics are FDA-approved and just as effective but cost 80-85% less.

2. Check your plan's formulary first

Before filling a prescription, verify it's covered and check the tier level.

3. Use mail-order pharmacy for maintenance medications

Get 90-day supplies for the price of 60 days through your insurance's mail service.

4. Compare prices at different pharmacies

GoodRx, Costco, and Walmart often have lower prices than traditional pharmacies.

5. Ask about patient assistance programs

Drug manufacturers often offer discounts or free medication for eligible patients.

6. Split higher-dose pills (if doctor approves)

A 20mg pill often costs the same as 10mg. Cut it in half and save 50%.

7. Use manufacturer coupons

Many brand-name drugs offer coupons on their websites that lower copays.

8. Request therapeutic alternatives

Ask your doctor if there's a similar medication in a lower tier that works equally well.

9. Don't automatically refill at the same pharmacy

Shop around each time‚Äîprices can vary significantly between pharmacies.
